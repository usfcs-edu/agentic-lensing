\subtitle{Phase 3a Internal Reproduction Report}

\begin{abstract}
We re-implement the \Lanusse{} \ResNet-46 architecture of
\textcite{lanusse2018cmu} in PyTorch and train it under the
\textcite{huang2020decals} recipe on $929$ NeuraLens L18-model lens
candidates plus $4{,}908$ random galaxies sampled from the DESI DR1
spectroscopic catalog as non-lenses. With no access to the original
training code, training set, or trained weights, we reach a held-out
test \AUC{} of $0.9991$ (validation \AUC{} $0.9983$) in $24.8$ minutes
on a single NVIDIA L4. This narrowly exceeds the published
\textcite{huang2020decals} validation \AUC{} of $0.98$ — but the
comparison is biased upward by two methodological substitutions
(NeuraLens catalog versus the historical Master Lens Database + 6
publications; uniformly random non-lens galaxies versus the paper's
curated by-eye hard negatives). We discuss the substitutions and
their effect in \S\ref{sec:caveats}, and signpost Phase 3b — full
DECaLS deployment to recover the published $335$ new candidates — as
the natural next step.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% =============================================================================

This report documents Phase 3a of an 8-phase reproduction roadmap covering
Prof. Xiaosheng Huang's strong-gravitational-lensing program at the
University of San Francisco. Phase 1 (\textcite{huang2025foundry1};
Foundry I HST observations + GIGA-Lens modeling) and Phase 2
\autocite{hsu2025pairwise} have already been reproduced in companion
tech-reports.\footnote{See \texttt{reproductions/foundry-i/papers/main.tex}
and \texttt{reproductions/hsu-2025/papers/main.tex}.} Phase 3 is the
ResNet/EfficientNet lens-finder lineage:
\textcite{huang2020decals,huang2021desi} and successors (Storfer 2024,
Inchausti 2025).

Phase 3a — this report — narrowly scopes \textcite{huang2020decals}: the
original 9{,}000~deg$^2$ DECaLS search that produced 335 new lens
candidates using the \Lanusse{} \ResNet{} of \textcite{lanusse2018cmu}.
The intent of Phase 3a is methodological validation: \emph{can we
re-implement the same architecture under the same hyperparameter recipe
from the paper alone and reproduce the published \AUC{} of $0.98$?}
Full deployment on a few million DEV/COMP-typed DECaLS galaxies to
re-discover the 335 candidates is Phase 3b, deferred to a separate
workstream.

The reproduction was performed without access to Huang's own training
code, training set, or trained weights. Once obtained through
collaboration, Huang's code will be the proper ground-truth comparison
target.

\section{Architecture}
% =============================================================================

We adopt the \Lanusse{} \ResNet-46 of \textcite{lanusse2018cmu} verbatim,
which is the same architecture \textcite{huang2020decals} re-implemented
in TensorFlow. The original is Theano/Lasagne; ours is PyTorch
(\textcite{paszke2019pytorch}; lives at
\texttt{reproductions/huang-2020/01\_lanusse\_resnet.py}).

The architecture (figure~\ref{fig:arch}) is one 7$\times$7 convolutional
stem followed by five stages of three pre-activated bottleneck residual
blocks each \autocite{he2016preact}. Each block has the form
$1{\times}1\,\to\,3{\times}3\,\to\,1{\times}1$ with ELU activation and
batch normalisation. Channel progression $32\to 32\to 64\to 128\to 256
\to 512$; spatial downsampling by 2 at the first block of every stage
except stage~1, achieved with strided convolutions in the bottleneck and
in the projection shortcut. The head is an adaptive average pool, a
single fully-connected layer to one logit, and a sigmoid. Total: 46
convolutional layers, $\sim$3.5~M parameters at 3-channel input.

\begin{figure}[ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/lanusse_resnet_arch.png}
\caption{Lanusse 2018 / CMU DeepLens \ResNet-46. The 5-stage
pre-activated bottleneck stack with channel progression $32\to 512$.
Output spatial resolutions are shown for a $101\times101$ input
(\textcite{huang2020decals} cutout size).}
\label{fig:arch}
\end{figure}

For the same architecture run at \textcite{lanusse2018cmu}'s native
$45\times45$ input, the spatial chain reduces to
$45\to 23\to 12\to 6\to 3$ which matches their Figure~4. A smoke test
in \texttt{01\_lanusse\_resnet.py} verifies a forward+backward pass at
both input sizes produces a real-valued logit, gradient-finite, with
parameter count $3{,}508{,}833$.

\section{Training set}
% =============================================================================

\paragraph{Positives.} 949 lens candidates from the NeuraLens combined
catalog\footnote{\url{https://drive.google.com/file/d/1_KbEHWhl8LeeTyXpXkWFbLRxt6o42wBg}},
filtered to \texttt{ResNet Model == 'L18'} (Huang+2020 era), parsed from
the \texttt{DESI-RA$\pm$DEC} naming convention. Of these, 929 have
successful \texttt{ls-dr9} cutouts on disk.

\paragraph{Negatives.} 5{,}000 random galaxies sampled from the
DESI DR1 redshift catalog
(\texttt{zall-pix-iron.fits}; \textcite{huang2025foundry1}) with the
filter cascade \texttt{ZCAT\_PRIMARY \&\& ZWARN==0 \&\& SPECTYPE!='STAR'
\&\& Z>0.05 \&\& Dec $\in [-68, +32.5]$}, with a 10''\ exclusion zone
around any positive. The expected lens-rate contamination is
$\sim 10^{-4}$ per \textcite{huang2020decals} \S3.1, so negligible
against 5{,}000 rows. After cutout download, $4{,}908$ negatives are
available.

\paragraph{Cutouts.} 101$\times$101 pixel grz, pixscale 0.262''/pix
(DECam native), FoV $\approx 26.5''$, pulled from
\texttt{legacysurvey.org/viewer/fits-cutout} on the \texttt{ls-dr9}
layer. Same field-of-view and band selection as Huang+2020 \S3.2.

\paragraph{Split.} Stratified 70:20:10 random shuffle by label, seed $2026$.
The resulting counts are shown in Table~\ref{tab:split}.

\textcite{huang2020decals} used $21{:}1$ (13{,}000 vs 613). This makes
our task moderately easier; we discuss the implication in
\S\ref{sec:caveats}.

\section{Hyperparameters}
% =============================================================================

Unchanged from \textcite{lanusse2018cmu} \S3.4 and \textcite{huang2020decals}
\S3.2:

\begin{itemize}
\item Optimizer: \Adam{} \autocite{kingma2015adam}, default
      $\beta_1=0.9, \beta_2=0.999, \epsilon=10^{-8}$.
\item Initial learning rate $1\times10^{-3}$, divided by 10 every 40
      epochs.
\item Batch size 128.
\item 120 epochs.
\item Loss: binary cross-entropy with logits (numerically stable form).
\item Preprocessing: per-band mean subtraction + per-band standard
      deviation normalisation, computed once over 500 randomly-sampled
      training cutouts; outputs clipped to $\pm 250\sigma$.
\item Augmentation (training-only): random rotation in $[-90, +90]^\circ$,
      random horizontal and vertical flips, random zoom in $[0.9, 1.0]$
      followed by centre-crop back to $101\times101$.
\end{itemize}

The only methodological deviation from the original is the rotation
implementation: PyTorch's \code{torchvision.transforms.functional.rotate}
with \texttt{InterpolationMode.BILINEAR} versus \textcite{lanusse2018cmu}'s
spline interpolation. We did not observe a difference attributable to
this in informal A/B tests.

\section{Training run}
% =============================================================================

Training was performed on a single NVIDIA L4 (24~GB) at
\texttt{/raid/benson/git/agentic-lensing}, using the PyTorch~2.12 +
\texttt{torchvision}~0.27 stack against CUDA~12.6 (venv at
\texttt{/raid/benson/.venvs/lensfinder}). Wall-clock for the full
120 epochs including held-out test pass was 24.8~min.
Figure~\ref{fig:curves} shows the loss and validation \AUC{} trajectories.

checkpoint lands at epoch 93. The final 40 epochs at
$10^{-5}$ provide a small additional refinement.

\section{Reproducibility caveats}
\label{sec:caveats}
% =============================================================================

The $0.9991$ test \AUC{} narrowly exceeds the $0.98$ published
\textcite{huang2020decals} validation \AUC{}, but a fair interpretation
must account for two methodological substitutions.

\paragraph{(i) The NeuraLens catalog mixes Huang's training inputs with
his discoveries.}
\textcite{huang2020decals} trained on $\sim$613 known lenses sourced from
the Master Lens Database \autocite{moustakas2012mld} plus six specific
recent publications (Carrasco 2017; Diehl 2017; Pourrahmani 2018;
Sonnenfeld 2018; Wong 2018; Jacobs 2017 \& 2019). The 949 L18-model rows
we use as positives are the \emph{output} of the trained classifier as
re-graded by the team: that is, approximately the 613 training inputs
\emph{plus} the 335 newly-discovered candidates, minus a handful of
re-grading rejections. The NeuraLens release table does not tag rows by
provenance, so we cannot split training-input from discovery-output
positives.

Consequence: our random 70:20:10 split places a fraction of the 93 test
positives at systems Huang himself trained on. Those systems tend to be
visually obvious well-curated lenses that survived MLD-style review —
substantially easier than typical sky candidates. The test \AUC{} is
therefore biased upward relative to a true out-of-sample evaluation.

\paragraph{(ii) Uniformly random DR1 galaxies are easy negatives.}
\textcite{huang2020decals} \S3.1 mentions an explicit curated set of
``by-eye'' hard negatives: spiral galaxies, galaxy groups, galaxies of
different colors, cosmic rays, unusual configurations, and data-
reduction artefacts (their Figure~2). These are the cases where a naive
classifier would produce false positives. We instead sampled negatives
uniformly from the DESI DR1 fiber catalog, which is dominated by smooth
elliptical and disk galaxies with no close companions — the easy
end of the discrimination problem.

Consequence: our model is being asked an easier yes/no question than the
paper's bench. A faithful reproduction at the same difficulty level
needs the by-eye hard-negative set; that's Phase 3b work.

\paragraph{Honest take.} Phase 3a establishes that the \emph{architecture
and training recipe} can be re-implemented to a working classifier from
the published methods alone. It does \emph{not} establish parity with
the paper's bench-marked performance. The $0.9991$ test \AUC{} should
be read as a lower bound on what is achievable, not as a like-for-like
improvement over $0.98$.

A proper closure of the Phase 3a $\to$ paper gap requires either
(a)~obtaining Huang's training set and code via collaboration, or
(b)~assembling an authentic 613-row MLD+publications training-positive
set \emph{and} a 13{,}000-row by-eye-hard-negative set, then re-running.

\section{Phase 3b: full DECaLS DR7 deployment}
\label{sec:phase3b}
% =============================================================================

We applied the Phase 3a checkpoint to the full DECaLS DR7 footprint and
quantified recovery of the 342 published candidates of
\textcite{huang2020decals}.

\subsection{Parent sample}
We downloaded all 292 DR7 sweep catalogs from the NERSC portal
(\verb|portal.nersc.gov/cfs/cosmo/data/legacysurvey/dr7/sweep/7.1/|;
$\approx$511\,GB), and applied the same selection criteria as
\textcite{huang2020decals} \S4.1: \verb|TYPE in {DEV, COMP}|,
$\verb|NOBS_G|, \verb|NOBS_R|, \verb|NOBS_Z| \geq 3$, and z-band
magnitude $\leq 20.0$. The resulting parent sample is
\textbf{6{,}242{,}507 galaxies} (88\% DEV, 12\% COMP) — within $\sim$10\%
of the paper's $5.7\,\mathrm{M}$.

\subsection{Brick-driven inference pipeline}
A na\"\i ve implementation that downloads cutouts from the
\verb|legacysurvey.org/viewer/fits-cutout| endpoint hits a
server-side bottleneck: cutout generation runs on demand, capping
throughput at $\sim$0.86\,rows/sec across 16 parallel workers
(extrapolation: $\sim$84 days for the full sweep). Instead, we
download each brick's grz image \verb|.fits.fz| files once
($3 \times \sim$15\,MB), load them with \verb|astropy|, project each
galaxy's (RA, Dec) to brick pixels via the brick WCS, and slice
$101\times 101$ cutouts locally. The two L4 GPUs each ran one shard,
splitting the 113{,}068 unique bricks by \verb|md5(BRICKNAME) % 2|.
Each shard processed $\sim$1.7\,bricks/sec ($\sim$100\,galaxies/sec
scored). The full sweep finished in 10\,h\,08\,m wall-clock (vs. an
extrapolated 84 days for the endpoint pipeline — a $\sim$\,200$\times$
speed-up).

\subsection{Headline result}
Of 6.24\,M scored galaxies, \textbf{74{,}011 have $p \geq 0.9$}
(vs.\ the paper's $\sim$50{,}000) and 56{,}483 have $p \geq 0.95$. To
cross-match against the published 342 candidates, we extracted their
DESI-RA$\pm$DEC names from Tables\,1--3 of \textcite{huang2020decals}
(60 Grade A, 106 Grade B, 176 Grade C) using \verb|pypdf|, then ran
a 5$''$-radius nearest-neighbour match in our parent sample.

\textbf{335 of the 342 published candidates fall inside our parent
sample} (the missing 7 sit at the type/magnitude cut margin in DR7).
Table~\ref{tab:phase3b-recovery} gives the recovery rate by grade and
threshold; Figure~\ref{fig:recovery-by-grade} visualises it.

\section{Phase 3b caveats}
\label{sec:caveats-phase3b}
% =============================================================================

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{DR7-vs-DR9 training shift.} Our Phase 3a model was trained
      on DR9 cutouts but Phase 3b inference is on DR7 (paper-exact).
      The smoke-test in \S\ref{sec:phase3b} confirmed all six sampled
      Grade-A candidates scored $\geq 0.946$ on DR7 (mean $0.984$), so
      the calibration shift between DR9 and DR7 is small. A future
      step could fine-tune on DR7 to close the remaining gap.
\item \textbf{Visual inspection is unfinished.} The paper's final
      342-candidate list was produced by four co-authors visually
      inspecting all $\sim$50{,}000 $p \geq 0.9$ cutouts, then two
      senior co-authors grading survivors A/B/C. Our 74{,}011-cutout
      pool would need similar human effort to convert score-ranked
      candidates into a final discovery list. The \texttt{cutouts\_fits\_dr7/}
      directory + a paginated HTML viewer in
      \texttt{papers/figures/inspection/} (M5) support this workflow,
      but we have not actually performed the visual grading at scale.
\item \textbf{The 7 ``missing'' published candidates.} 7/342 fall just
      outside our parent sample. The most likely reason is that their
      DR7 type or magnitude differs slightly from the paper-time
      catalog (e.g., subsequent Tractor reprocessing reclassified
      \texttt{DEV $\to$ REX}). The published catalog is therefore not
      fully derivable from a single static DR7 snapshot.
\item \textbf{Grade-C recovery is moderate ($43.8\%$ at $p \geq 0.9$).}
      Grade C candidates have small/faint deflections close to the
      seeing; our random-DR9-negative training set under-represents
      this regime relative to the paper's by-eye hard negatives, which
      may explain the gap.
\end{itemize}

% =============================================================================
\section{Conclusions}
% =============================================================================

We implemented and trained a PyTorch port of the
\textcite{lanusse2018cmu} \ResNet-46 under the
\textcite{huang2020decals} hyperparameter recipe, reaching a test
\AUC{} of $0.9991$ (Phase 3a, \S\ref{sec:caveats}), then deployed the
trained checkpoint on the full DECaLS DR7 footprint (Phase 3b,
\S\ref{sec:phase3b}). The deployment recovers \textbf{$90.0\%$ of
Grade A and $59.6\%$ overall} of the 342 \textcite{huang2020decals}
published candidates at the paper's reported $p \geq 0.9$ threshold,
in 10\,h on two L4 GPUs end-to-end. The architecture, the loss, the
optimiser settings, the data augmentation, the cutout size, the
deployment cuts, and the inference threshold are all faithful to the
published descriptions. The remaining gap (cf.\ Grade C recovery at
$43.8\%$) is consistent with our smaller and more uniformly-distributed
training set; closing it would require the paper's by-eye-curated hard
negatives, which were not publicly released.

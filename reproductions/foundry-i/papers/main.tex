\begin{abstract}
We present an end-to-end, public-data reproduction of the GIGA-Lens
modeling demonstration for the DESI Strong Lens Foundry I system
\foundry, originally reported by \citet{huang2025foundry1}.
Starting from the same {\it Hubble Space Telescope}\ WFC3/F140W exposure
(GO--15867; MAST DOI 10.17909/hx0v-9260) and using only open-source
infrastructure --- \gigalens, \lenstronomy, \photutils, JAX, and
\textsc{TensorFlow Probability} --- we rebuild the data products,
construct an empirical PSF from in-field stars, and fit an EPL$+$external
shear mass model together with four S\'{e}rsic lens-light components,
a S\'{e}rsic source, and a shapelet basis of order $n_{\max}=6$
($74$ free non-linear parameters). The naive gigalens \texttt{HMC()}
helper did not JIT-compile at this model size under JAX\,0.6.2; we trace
this to a \texttt{pmap}-over-all-ten-devices wrapper, a $31$-channel
grouped \texttt{lstsq} convolution, and cuDNN autotuner thrashing, and
show that with single-device execution, autotuning disabled, and a
smooth marginalized target, genuine HMC compiles in $\sim$35\,s and
samples the correct mode. For the headline result we nonetheless report
the v10 Gaussian stochastic variational posterior (initialized from the
best paper-mode chain of a 200-chain multi-start MAP search); a v11 NUTS
run started from the v7 paper-mode MAP point confirms posterior
multimodality and locates a third distinct local mode. A subsequent HMC
investigation (\S\ref{sec:hmc}) further corrects a saddle in the
paper-mode MAP ($\Delta\log p\approx+62{,}000$), introduces an exact
regularized Gaussian marginalization of the linear light amplitudes that
fixes a non-smoothness in gigalens \texttt{lstsq\_simulate}, and shows
that the residual sampling difficulty is degeneracy-limited mixing
($\hat R>1$ at $500$ samples, $\mathrm{cond}\sim10^{8}$) rather than a
sampler defect; in our setup the paper's slope $\gammaEPL=1.372$ is a
distinct, far worse fit ($\Delta\log p\approx-32{,}500$), pointing to an
unpublished model-setup difference. Our 10\,000-sample variational posterior recovers
all six sign quadrants of the mass model, matches \thetaE\ to 3.0\%,
$e_{1}$ to 2.5\%, and the external shear position angle to within
$1^{\circ}$ of the published HMC result; the v11f NUTS mode reproduces
\thetaE\ to 1.6\%, the closest of any version to date. The remaining
gap --- a $12^{\circ}$ rotation of the lens ellipticity vector,
$\sim30\%$ smaller $|\etwo|$ and $|\gext|$, and a $57\%$ steeper
deprojected slope $\gammaEPL=2.15$ versus the published $1.37$ --- is
shown to be coherent with a single missing constraint, plausibly a
mass--light position-angle tie or higher-order multipole correction.
We document upstream issues in \gigalens\,2.0/JAX\,0.6.2/TFP\,0.25.0
suitable for filing as GitHub issues, and provide the full software
stack table to support future Foundry-series reproductions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

Strong gravitational lensing by galaxies is one of the rare astrophysical
phenomena that maps directly to a small number of free physical
parameters --- the projected mass profile of the deflector, the
source position and morphology, and (in some configurations) the
cosmological distance ratio $D_{ls}/D_{s}$ --- and yet produces
information-rich images whose deflections are sensitive to all of them
simultaneously \citep{refsdal1964,treu2010annurev}. With the
{\it Vera C. Rubin Observatory}\ Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST),
the {\it Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope}, and {\it Euclid}\ now
delivering or imminently delivering wide-field imaging at depth, the
number of known galaxy-scale strong lenses is set to grow from
$\mathcal{O}(10^{3})$ today to $\mathcal{O}(10^{5})$ within a decade.
The bottleneck for cosmological and population-level analyses is no
longer discovery but {\it modeling at scale}.

\gigalens\ \citep{gu2022gigalens} was designed to break that
bottleneck. It expresses the forward lens-model likelihood as a
differentiable JAX/TensorFlow program, exploits hardware parallelism
through \texttt{pmap}/\texttt{shard\_map}, and replaces traditional MCMC
with a hybrid pipeline: stochastic variational inference (SVI) for fast,
initial posterior exploration followed by short Hamiltonian Monte Carlo
chains for refinement. On modern GPU hardware \gigalens\ has been
demonstrated to reduce per-system modeling times from days to minutes,
which is the throughput required to model the LSST/Roman/Euclid lens
catalog within a single graduate-student-year of effort.

The DESI Strong Lens Foundry program was conceived as a follow-up
imaging and modeling campaign for promising lens candidates discovered
in the DESI Legacy Imaging Surveys
\citep{huang2020decals,huang2021desi}. Foundry I
\citep{huang2025foundry1}, the focus of the present paper, is the
first in the series: it presents {\it HST}\ WFC3/F140W observations of
ten high-priority systems (GO--15867; PI Huang) and uses one system,
\foundry, as the methodological demonstration. The Foundry I
\gigalens\ recipe --- an EPL$+$external shear deflector, a S\'{e}rsic
plus shapelet source, and an HMC posterior --- has since been propagated
to other GIGA-Lens-lineage modeling papers, including the
DESI--253.2534$+$26.8843 Einstein-cross analysis of
\citet{cikota2023einsteincross} and the multi-source Carousel-Lens
work of \citet{sheu2024carousel}, as well as forthcoming
Foundry II--V volumes.

Reproducibility of such an end-to-end pipeline --- from MAST query to
HMC posterior --- is non-trivial. The published Foundry I posterior is
a Markov chain in a 74-dimensional space, conditioned on a TinyTim PSF,
a custom central pixel mask, and a non-trivial set of priors
(\citealt{huang2025foundry1} Table~2). A modest discrepancy in any
single element of the recipe can shift the posterior by several formal
$\sigma$ along directions the chain itself was constructed to explore.
For the broader Foundry-series program, and indeed for any future
LSST/Roman/Euclid effort that adopts \gigalens-style differentiable
modeling, an open and {\it independently}\ reproduced version of the
pipeline is a useful complement to the original results: it
exposes which assumptions are load-bearing, which choices are
load-light, and how the published posterior responds to perturbations
that the authors themselves could not run.

In this paper we report such a reproduction. Section~\ref{sec:data}
describes how we re-pull the {\it HST}\ data from MAST. Section
\ref{sec:psf} documents an empirical-PSF pipeline that we find
necessary for the reproduction to converge to the paper's posterior
mode. Section~\ref{sec:model} sets up the lens model and priors.
Section~\ref{sec:inference} describes our inference pipeline, the
HMC compile-time obstacle that forced us to fall back on the SVI
Gaussian variational posterior, and a subsequent v11 NUTS attempt that
resolves the JIT-compile blocker but does not yield a mixed chain.
Section~\ref{sec:results} presents the quantitative comparison to
\citet{huang2025foundry1} Table~3 and addresses the multimodality we
observed in the multi-start MAP search and (separately) in the v11
NUTS run. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} interprets the residual systematic
and itemizes upstream software issues. Section~\ref{sec:software}
documents the software stack. Appendix~\ref{app:ablation} traces the
internal ablation v1\,$\to$\,v11 that led to the working recipe;
Appendix~\ref{app:priors} reproduces the prior table.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The Foundry I system \foundry\ was observed with {\it HST}/WFC3-IR in the
F140W filter under program GO--15867 (PI Huang). The combined drizzled
exposure used in this work has a total integration of $1197.7\,$s in
three exposures of $399.23\,$s, was processed through the standard
WFC3 calibration pipeline, and is available from MAST under DOI
10.17909/hx0v-9260. We retrieved the high-level pipeline-combined
drizzle product \texttt{hst\_15867\_65\_wfc3\_ir\_f140w\_ie5065\_drz.fits}
via the \texttt{astroquery.mast.Observations} interface.

The full drizzled science extension is $1523\times1528$ pixels at the
WFC3-IR native pixel scale of $0\farcs13\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$. We cut a
$128\times128$ box centered on
$(\alpha,\delta)=(165\fdg4754,-6\fdg0423)$, the source position
given by \citet{huang2025foundry1}. We sky-subtract by the median of
the cutout and adopt a per-pixel noise floor
$\sigma_{\mathrm{bg}}\approx0.029\,\mathrm{e^{-}\,s^{-1}}$ from the
inverse-variance \texttt{WHT} extension. Figure~\ref{fig:cutout} shows
the sky-subtracted science image with an asinh stretch alongside the
\texttt{WHT} extension and a logarithmic display.

\section{Empirical PSF Construction}
\label{sec:psf}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

The PSF kernel is the second-most-critical input to a differentiable
lensing forward model, after the data itself. The \gigalens\
distribution ships with a TinyTim WFC3/F140W kernel of shape
$13\times13$ at $0\farcs065\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$, i.e. with $\pm
0\farcs42$ support. We found this kernel to be too compact for the
present analysis (\S\ref{sec:results}), and built an empirical PSF
from in-field stars in the same exposure, following the approach
described by \citet{huang2025foundry1}.

The pipeline (script \texttt{17\_build\_empirical\_psf.py}) is:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Open the full $1523\times1528$ drizzled science extension.
Compute sigma-clipped background statistics with
\texttt{astropy.stats.sigma\_clipped\_stats} ($\sigma=3$).
\item Detect bright sources with \texttt{photutils.detection.DAOStarFinder}
at FWHM $=3\,$pix, threshold $50\sigma_{\mathrm{bg}}$, with sharpness
in $[0.2,1.0]$ and roundness in $[-0.5,0.5]$.
\item Reject candidates within $200\,$pix of the lens centroid and
within $25\,$pix of any image edge; sort the remainder by peak flux
and retain the top 30.
\item Pass these to \texttt{photutils.psf.EPSFBuilder}
\citep{bradley2024photutils} with $2\times$ oversampling
($0\farcs065\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$ effective), the quartic smoothing
kernel, eight outer iterations, and 20 recentering iterations per
build.
\item Crop the resulting effective PSF to $27\times27$ supersampled
pixels (a tight box of $\pm0\farcs85$ on a side) and normalize to unit
sum.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:psf} shows our empirical PSF beside the gigalens-default
TinyTim kernel and the median-stacked top-9 raw star postage stamps.
The empirical kernel is broader than TinyTim by roughly a factor of
two in the core, and carries finite power out to the $\pm0\farcs85$
radius --- a region in which TinyTim is identically zero. We will
return to the implications of this width difference in
\S\ref{sec:results}.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/psf_comparison.png}
\caption{Empirical PSF construction. Left to right: median of the
top-9 raw F140W star postage stamps; the $27\times27$ supersampled
($0\farcs065\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$) empirical PSF built by

\end{figure*}

\section{Lens Model}
\label{sec:model}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We adopt the same parametric lens model as \citet{huang2025foundry1}:

\subsection{Mass}
A single elliptical power-law convergence
(\texttt{EPL}, \citealt{gu2022gigalens,birrer2018lenstronomy}) with
Einstein radius \thetaE, deprojected 3D mass-density slope \gammaEPL,
ellipticity $(\eone,\etwo)$, and centroid
$(c_{x}^{\mathrm{mass}},c_{y}^{\mathrm{mass}})$,
plus an external shear with two Cartesian components
$(\gextone,\gexttwo)$. This is 8 free mass parameters.

\subsection{Lens light}
Four elliptical S\'{e}rsic profiles with
\texttt{use\_lstsq=False} (i.e.\ the per-component intensity
$I_{e}$ is sampled as a free parameter rather than recovered by an
unconstrained least-squares solver). Two of the four model the central
deflector itself (one compact and one extended); the remaining two
model a fainter companion galaxy detected at $(-2\farcs34,-2\farcs86)$
relative to the main-lens centroid in the v2 residual map
(Figure~\ref{fig:nearby}, see \S\ref{sec:results}). Allowing
$I_{e}$ to be negative through an unconstrained least-squares pass
turned out to be a critical pitfall: an unconstrained fit can drive
some $I_{e}$ negative and absorb $\chi^{2}$ at non-physical
configurations, lowering Einstein-radius accuracy from $0.5\%$ to
$43\%$ (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}, v4 versus v5).

\subsection{Source light}
A single elliptical S\'{e}rsic plus a Cartesian shapelet basis of
maximum order $n_{\max}=6$ \citep{gu2022gigalens}, giving 28 amplitude
coefficients in addition to the basis-scale parameter $\beta$ and the
shapelet centroid. With \texttt{use\_lstsq=False} we sample the
amplitudes explicitly under a per-coefficient
$\mathcal{N}(0,\,5/\sqrt{i+1})$ prior, with the order-decaying scale
adopted from the canonical \gigalens\ shapelets demonstration. Free-sign
sampling here is appropriate (shapelet basis coefficients can be of
either sign by construction) and complementary to the positive-sign
constraint on lens-light $I_{e}$.

\subsection{Central mask}
\citet{huang2025foundry1} adopt a 2.5-pixel circular mask at the
drizzled $0\farcs065\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$ scale to exclude the
central pixels where the lens-light $\rightarrow$ source-light
decomposition is degenerate. Our model lives on the WFC3-IR native
$0\farcs13\,\mathrm{pix}^{-1}$ grid, so we mask the 1.5-pixel circle
($\approx0\farcs20$ radius). The mask is implemented as an inflation
of the per-pixel error map \texttt{err\_map} to $10^{10}$ inside the
mask --- this is the smallest change to \gigalens\,2.0 that yields a
masked likelihood, because the upstream
\texttt{gigalens.jax.model.ForwardProbModel} class does not yet
expose a mask argument. The full custom \texttt{masked\_log\_prob}
function is monkey-patched into the prob model as a JIT-compiled
method.

\subsection{Free-parameter count}
Mass: 8. Lens light (S\'{e}rsic): $4\times7=28$.
Source S\'{e}rsic: 7. Source shapelets: $1+2+28=31$. Total: 74 free
non-linear parameters. The full prior specification is given in
Table~\ref{tab:priors} (Appendix~\ref{app:priors}).

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{figures/nearby_galaxy_detection.png}
\caption{Detection of the nearby galaxy on the v2 residual map.
A clear point-like positive residual at
$(\Delta\alpha,\Delta\delta)\approx(-2\farcs34,-2\farcs86)$ from
the main-lens centroid is the signature of an unmodeled companion;
including its light as two additional S\'{e}rsic components

\section{Inference Pipeline}
\label{sec:inference}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

\subsection{Hardware}
All fits ran on \texttt{/raid/benson}, an AlmaLinux 9.7 aarch64 host
with two NVIDIA L4 (23\,GB) and eight NVIDIA A16 (15\,GB) GPUs visible
to CUDA. \gigalens's \texttt{shard\_map}-parallelised
\texttt{ModellingSequence.MAP} and \texttt{.SVI} routines build their
compiled executables for a single GPU type and then attempt to dispatch
across all visible devices, which fails on this host with a
\texttt{cannot run on CUDA:2 of type 'NVIDIA A16'} error. We work
around this by UUID-pinning the two L4s via
\texttt{CUDA\_VISIBLE\_DEVICES=GPU-$\langle$uuid-of-L4-0$\rangle$,GPU-$\langle$uuid-of-L4-1$\rangle$}.
Once pinned, JIT compilation succeeds and the two L4s deliver
$\sim$1.7 steps/s on the 200-chain, 400-step v9 MAP problem
(\S\ref{sec:results}), or $\sim$3 minutes of wall time after JIT.

\subsection{MAP search (Stage A)}
We run a 200-chain Adabelief \citep[][lr$=10^{-2}$, $b_{1}=0.95$,
$b_{2}=0.99$]{birrer2018lenstronomy} multi-start MAP search over
400 steps. Each chain is seeded by an independent prior sample.
\texttt{ModellingSequence.MAP(\dots,\,output\_type='best')} returns the
single chain with the highest log posterior at the final step. Crucially
we also retain the full
\texttt{(n\_chains,\,n\_steps,\,n\_params)} array so that we can
characterize the multimodality of the posterior surface; this is the
v7/v9 multi-start analysis of \S\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{SVI (Stage B)}
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, attempted naively as the gigalens
\texttt{HMC()} helper, did not complete in any reasonable wall time:
the JIT compile of
\texttt{pmap(scan(mcmc\_sample\_chain(PreconditionedHMC} $+$
\texttt{GradientBasedTrajectoryLengthAdaptation)))} did not finish
within several hours on the present 74-parameter model under
JAX 0.6.2 \citep{bradbury2018jax}. For the headline result of this
paper we therefore adopt a Gaussian stochastic variational posterior as
a surrogate. This is documented honestly throughout
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}): the Table~\ref{tab:mass} headline is SVI, not
HMC, and our $1\sigma$ widths there are {\it variational}-posterior
widths (and therefore underestimates of the marginal posterior width if
the true posterior is non-Gaussian in the parameters of interest). We
emphasize, however, that the compile failure was a {\it configuration}\
problem, not a fundamental limit: \S\ref{sec:hmc} reports a subsequent
investigation in which genuine HMC compiles in $\sim$35\,s, runs stably,
and samples the correct mode once the \texttt{pmap}-over-all-devices,
\texttt{lstsq}-convolution, and autotuner pathologies are removed and a
smooth, marginalized target is used.

The SVI is initialised at the best paper-mode chain found by the
preceding 200-chain MAP search (\texttt{map\_v9\_paper\_mode.npz}),
with $n_{\mathrm{VI}}=200$ particles, an Adabelief learning rate of
$10^{-4}$, init scales of $10^{-4}$, and 500 steps. The tight
initialisation and the small learning rate are intentional: they pin
the variational posterior to the paper-mode local minimum rather than
allowing it to drift to one of the higher-$\chi^{2}$ but lower-log-$p$
modes that we identified in v7 (\S\ref{sec:results}).

After 500 SVI steps the negative ELBO has fallen monotonically from
$5.69\times10^{4}$ to $4.58\times10^{4}$; the chain shows no sign of
mode-hopping, and the final variational mean is essentially indistinguishable
from the initial MAP. We then draw $10^{4}$ samples from $q(z)$ and
push them through the bijector to recover the physical-parameter
posterior used in Table~\ref{tab:mass}.

\subsection{Wall time}
Total wall time for the full v9 multi-start MAP plus v10 SVI is
$\approx7\,$min ($3.9\,$min for MAP, $4.9\,$min for SVI), excluding
the one-time JIT compile (which dominates the first run; for the SVI
specifically, cuDNN convolution autotuning takes $\sim 90\,$s due to
the $f32[100,1,256,256]$ convolution shape required by the $74$-parameter
model on 2 GPUs).

\subsection{NUTS attempt (v11 series)}
\label{sec:nuts}

Having identified the HMC JIT compile-time blowup as the most
load-bearing of our upstream issues (\S\ref{sec:discussion}), we
attempted a sequence of six NUTS runs (v11, v11b, v11c, v11d-scan,
v11e, v11f) targeting that issue specifically. We treat the resulting
chain as a {\it diagnostic}, not a replacement headline posterior;
the v10 SVI fit of \S\ref{sec:headline} remains the canonical result.

\paragraph{NUTS does compile.} The replacement we adopt is
\texttt{tfp.experimental.mcmc.PreconditionedNoUTurnSampler} (TFP\,0.25.0,
JAX backend) with \texttt{max\_tree\_depth=6} pinned as a static argument
and {\it without}\ a \texttt{pmap} wrapper. With these two choices the
sampler JIT-compiles in $\sim$5\,min on the same 2$\times$L4 host that
failed to compile HMC after several hours, and runs 700 chain steps
(200 burn-in $+$ 500 kept) in $\sim$2--3\,min of wall time. This is
the main positive finding of the v11 series: the JIT-tractability of
sample-chain at $74$ parameters under JAX\,0.6.2 is recovered by NUTS
$+$ static tree depth, even though the same combinatorics defeated
\texttt{PreconditionedHMC}$+$\texttt{Gra\-dient\-Based\-Tra\-jec\-tory\-Length\-Adap\-ta\-tion}.

\paragraph{The v10 SVI covariance is rank-deficient.} On loading
\texttt{svi\_v10\_posterior\_mass.npz} and computing the SVI posterior
covariance in $z$-space, we find that \texttt{np.linalg.eigvalsh(qz\_cov)}
reports a minimum eigenvalue of $-4.4\times10^{-13}$ --- numerically zero
but slightly negative --- and a numerical rank of $53/74$
(\texttt{np.linalg.matrix\_rank}, default tolerance), implying $\sim 21$
near-degenerate directions. The CPU NumPy Cholesky
(\texttt{np.linalg.cholesky}) raises \texttt{NotPositiveDefinite}; the
JAX Cholesky (\texttt{jnp.linalg.cholesky}) {\it silently returns NaN}.
The first NUTS attempt (v11) used this raw covariance as the
\texttt{precondition\_state\_param\_partial\_loc} and inherited NaN
momentum samples; TFP's NUTS proceeds without erroring and produces
near-zero proposals. The fix is to jitter the covariance by
$10^{-6}\,\mathbb{I}$ before Cholesky, after which the Cholesky
diagonal lies in $[1.0\times10^{-3},6.2\times10^{-2}]$ and is usable.

\paragraph{Dual-averaging step-size collapse.} Even with a valid
(jittered) preconditioner (v11e), TFP's
\texttt{DualAveragingStepSizeAdaptation} consistently drove the step
size to $\sim$2--$3\times10^{-6}$ by the end of the 200-step burn-in,
at which point every proposed move is effectively the current state
(acceptance probability $1.000$, leapfrog count clamped at the
$2^{6}-1=63$ maximum, and per-step displacement $\|\Delta z\|\sim
10^{-4}$--$10^{-3}$ in unconstrained space). A static step-size scan
over $\{10^{-5},3\times10^{-5},10^{-4},3\times10^{-4},10^{-3},
3\times10^{-3},10^{-2},3\times10^{-2}\}$ with adaptation disabled
(v11d-scan) gave 0\% acceptance for every entry $\ge10^{-5}$ --- the
chain immediately U-turns and rejects --- and reported
\texttt{leapfrogs\_taken=1} at every iteration. A working step size
therefore exists somewhere in the interval $(2\times10^{-6},10^{-5})$,
but neither simple dual-averaging nor a coarse log-spaced scan locates
it; the local Hessian along the SVI mode and the local Hessian along
the basin we actually want to explore appear to differ enough that a
single global mass matrix is not adequate.

\paragraph{A third local mode (v11f).} As a final attempt, we
re-initialized NUTS from the v7 paper-mode MAP chain
(\texttt{map\_v7\_paper\_mode.npz}; $\log p = -21{,}789$ under TinyTim
PSF, but $\log p = -99{,}119$ under the empirical PSF used here)
rather than the v10 SVI mean, kept the $10^{-6}\,\mathbb{I}$ jitter,
and loosened the dual-averaging target acceptance from the default
$0.8$ to $0.6$. The chain moves $\sim 35{,}000$ $\log p$ units during
burn-in into a {\it new}\ local mode with $\log p \approx -63{,}868$,
distinct from both the v10 SVI mode ($\log p \approx -40{,}400$) and
the original paper-mode MAP. The posterior medians at this new mode
are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:nuts_v11f}. With $\thetaE=2.604''$,
v11f reproduces the published Einstein radius to $1.6\%$ --- the
closest of any version of this reproduction --- although the other
five mass parameters move in mixed directions relative to the v10
medians. We emphasize that v11f is a {\it converged-to-a-different-basin}\
point estimate, not a mixed chain; its $1\sigma$ widths
(Table~\ref{tab:nuts_v11f}) are widths of a chain that froze and are
not faithful marginal-posterior widths.

\paragraph{Why we did not pursue \texttt{windowed\_adaptive\_nuts}.}
The natural next step is TFP's \texttt{tfp.experimental.mcmc.
windowed\_adaptive\_nuts}, which runs Stan-style window-adaptive mass
matrix estimation interleaved with step-size adaptation and would in
principle resolve the curvature mismatch between modes. The blocker
is interface: \texttt{windowed\_adaptive\_nuts} expects a
\texttt{tfd.JointDistribution\-Auto\-Batched} or equivalent, while the
\gigalens\ \texttt{ForwardProbModel} exposes
\texttt{log\_prob}/\texttt{sample} but not the joint-distribution
abstraction. Refactoring the prob model to expose a
\texttt{JointDistribution} interface is straightforward in principle
but well-scoped enough to defer to a follow-up paper; the present
work documents the obstacle so that downstream users know what would
be required.

\subsection{Genuine HMC: compile diagnosis, mode correction,
            and a mixing recipe}
\label{sec:hmc}

The v11 NUTS series above (\S\ref{sec:nuts}) was a stop-gap: it
established that a gradient-based sampler {\it can}\ run at this model
size, but it never produced a mixed chain. We subsequently carried out
a focused investigation into whether {\it Hamiltonian Monte Carlo
itself}\ --- the sampler the Foundry I recipe actually specifies --- can
be made to compile, stay numerically stable, and sample the correct
posterior on this system. The short answer is yes: every prior pathology
we attributed to ``HMC not working'' turned out to be a fixable
mechanical or numerical bug, and after fixing them HMC compiles in
$\sim$35\,s, runs stably, and samples a smooth, near-positive-definite
mode at the correct posterior scale. What remains is a genuine
{\it mixing-efficiency}\ problem set by the intrinsic parameter
degeneracies of the lens model, not by the sampler. We document the
investigation here because each step is a reusable lesson for
\gigalens-style differentiable modeling at this dimensionality. The
scripts are \texttt{26\_compile\_diagnostic.py},
\texttt{28\_refine\_map.py}, \texttt{30\_refine\_lstsq.py},
\texttt{32\_saddlefree\_newton.py}, \texttt{33\_trust\_refine.py}, and
\texttt{34\_fit\_marg.py}, with the target-density libraries
\texttt{\_hmc\_lib.py} (full $74$-parameter model),
\texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_lstsq.py} (\texttt{lstsq}-profiled), and
\texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_marg.py} (regularized Gaussian marginalization).

\paragraph{(1) The compile blowup is configuration, not a time budget.}
We AOT-lowered and compiled the {\it one-step}\ kernel
(\texttt{bootstrap\_results}$+$\texttt{one\_step} inside a single
\texttt{jax.jit}) for three samplers on a single device
(\texttt{26\_compile\_diagnostic.py}). Every kernel compiles in
$31$--$38$\,s, {\it including}\ the gigalens stack
\texttt{Preconditioned\-Hamiltonian\-Monte\-Carlo}$\,\to\,$
\texttt{Gradient\-Based\-Tra\-jec\-tory\-Length\-Adap\-ta\-tion} (GBTLA)
$\,\to\,$\texttt{DualAveragingStepSizeAdaptation} that we had previously
reported as ``does not compile.'' The original multi-hour hang was
therefore {\it not}\ a compile-time budget problem. It was the
combination of (i) gigalens's \texttt{HMC()} helper wrapping the GBTLA
kernel in \texttt{jax.pmap} over {\it all}\ 10 visible devices, (ii) the
\texttt{use\_lstsq=True} path generating a 31-channel grouped
convolution (\texttt{conv\_general\_dilated} with
\texttt{feature\_group\_count}$=$depth, HLO shape
$f32[10,31,256,256]$), and (iii) the cuDNN convolution autotuner
thrashing across algorithms for that shape --- one candidate algorithm
alone took $11$\,min\,$50$\,s. The fix is to run on a single (or a few)
devices and to disable autotuning with
\texttt{XLA\_FLAGS=--xla\_gpu\_autotune\_level=0}. The single-device
diagnostic is a strict lower bound on the original $10$-device,
\texttt{lstsq}-convolution pathology; with these two changes the kernel
compiles in well under a minute.

\paragraph{(2) The ``paper-mode MAP'' we had been starting from is a
saddle.} Every prior run (the v8/v10 SVI, the v11f NUTS) was
initialised at the v7 ``paper-mode MAP'' point. The Laplace Hessian of
$-\log p$ at that point has $15$ {\it large negative}\ eigenvalues: it
is a saddle, not a maximum, and one cannot mix an HMC chain from a
saddle. Driving the gradient down with a strong optimizer followed by a
damped-Newton polish (\texttt{28\_refine\_map.py}) removes the saddle and
improves the log-posterior by $\approx +62{,}000$ (from
$\log p\approx-99{,}126$ at the v7 point under the empirical PSF to
$\approx-36{,}750$ at the refined point). This is a correction to the
earlier sections of this reproduction: the MAP we had been treating as
``the'' paper mode was not even a local maximum of our own posterior.

\paragraph{(3a) Flat directions from the linear light amplitudes.} The
$74$-parameter model samples $33$ {\it linear}\ light amplitudes ($5$
S\'{e}rsic $I_{e}$ plus $28$ shapelet coefficients) explicitly. At the
refined mode these create $\sim 56$ near-flat Hessian directions and cap
the effective sample size at ESS\,$\sim$\,$4$ no matter how the momentum
is preconditioned. Marginalizing the linear amplitudes out of the sampled
space --- the gigalens \texttt{lstsq} idea, here as the reduced
$41$-nonlinear-parameter model in \texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_lstsq.py}
(\texttt{build\_model\_lstsq}, using the gigalens \texttt{BackwardProbModel}
and \texttt{lstsq\_simulate}) --- removes the flat directions and shifts
$\gammaEPL$ from $2.18$ to $1.90$.

\paragraph{(3b) The reduced objective is stiff and needs float64.} The
amplitude-profiled objective is ill-conditioned ($\mathrm{cond}\gtrsim
10^{9}$). In float32 the gradient norm $\|\nabla(-\log p)\|$ never falls
below $\sim 1.2\times10^{4}$; refinement stalls on rounding error.
Enabling \texttt{jax\_enable\_x64} (float64 throughout
\texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_marg.py}, gated on \texttt{GIGALENS\_X64=1} before the
first array is created) is required for any of the downstream
mode-finding to proceed.

\paragraph{(3c) The key methodological bug: gigalens
\texttt{lstsq\_simulate} is non-smooth.} Even in float64, the
\texttt{lstsq}-profiled objective floors at $\|\nabla(-\log p)\|\sim
10^{5}$, stays persistently indefinite, and admits no positive-definite
mode --- a saddle-free Newton solver (\texttt{32\_saddlefree\_newton.py})
and a SciPy \texttt{trust-exact} refinement (\texttt{33\_trust\_refine.py})
both stall there. The cause is in gigalens itself: \texttt{lstsq\_simulate}
(\texttt{gigalens.jax.simulator}) solves the linear amplitudes through
$\mathrm{pinv}(X^{\!\top}WX)$ and (a) {\it omits}\ the Gaussian-evidence
normalization $-\tfrac12\log\det(X^{\!\top}WX)$ and (b) is
{\it unregularized}. On the near-rank-deficient $28$-column shapelet
design matrix $X$, the \texttt{pinv} singular-value truncation makes the
profiled log-posterior {\it non-smooth}\ in the nonlinear parameters
$\theta$: the truncation boundary moves discontinuously as $\theta$
changes, so \texttt{jax.grad} sees a noise floor rather than a true
gradient. No amount of float64 or mass-matrix tuning can fix a
non-smooth target.

\paragraph{(3d) The fix: exact, regularized Gaussian marginalization.}
We marginalize the $28$ shapelet amplitudes {\it analytically}, using
their own Gaussian priors $\mathcal{N}(0,\,5/\sqrt{i+1})$ as a Tikhonov
ridge (\texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_marg.py}, \texttt{build\_model\_marg}). Writing
$W$ for the per-pixel noise precision, $X$ for the lensed, PSF-convolved,
downsampled unit-amplitude shapelet design matrix, and $R$ for the
residual after the deterministic (S\'{e}rsic) image, the amplitude solve
becomes
\begin{align*}
  A &= X^{\!\top} W X + \Lambda, \qquad
       \Lambda_{ii} = \frac{1}{\sigma_i^{2}} = \frac{i+1}{25},\\
  b &= X^{\!\top}(W\!\odot R), \qquad
       a^{\star} = A^{-1} b \;=\; \mathrm{cho\_solve}(A,\,b),\\
  \log L &= -\tfrac12\!\sum W R^{2}
            + \tfrac12\, b^{\!\top} a^{\star}
            - \tfrac12 \log\det(A).
\end{align*}
Because the ridge makes $A$ strictly positive-definite, the amplitude
solve is a Cholesky solve (never \texttt{pinv}) and the evidence term
$-\tfrac12\log\det A$ --- the very Occam term gigalens omits --- is a
smooth \texttt{slogdet}. The remaining sampled space is $46$ parameters:
the $41$ nonlinear parameters plus the $5$ S\'{e}rsic $I_{e}$, which are
sampled under strictly-positive LogNormal priors rather than
marginalized (they are well-constrained and physical). This single change
broke the gradient floor: $\|\nabla(-\log p)\|$ dropped from
$\sim 9\times10^{4}$ to $\sim 4\times10^{2}$ (a $\sim$$228\times$
reduction), and the Hessian at the refined point became positive in
$44$ of $46$ directions. We report this as an upstream gigalens issue
(\S\ref{sec:upstream}): \texttt{lstsq\_simulate} should add the
$-\tfrac12\log\det$ evidence term and a prior-derived ridge.

\paragraph{(4) Mass matrix and the residual conditioning.} The smooth,
near-PD residual posterior is nonetheless {\it extremely}\
ill-conditioned ($\mathrm{cond}\sim10^{14}$): the companion-galaxy
lens-light S\'{e}rsic {\it centers}\ (LL2/LL3) have Hessian curvature
$H_{ii}\sim10^{12}$, while the S\'{e}rsic {\it indices}\ $n$ sit at
$H_{ii}\sim10^{-2}$; the physical mass parameters
($\thetaE,\gammaEPL,e_1,e_2$, shear) occupy a well-behaved
$10^{8}$--$10^{9}$ tier in between. We tried three momentum covariances
(\texttt{34\_fit\_marg.py --massmatrix}). (i) The PD-floored full Hessian
(\texttt{hess\_marg\_pd}) over-constrains the soft directions --- it
freezes $\gammaEPL$ to a posterior width of $\sim 10^{-4}$, which is
unphysically narrow. (ii) An {\it un-floored diagonal}\ mass matrix built
directly from $|H_{ii}|$ (\texttt{diagraw}; float64-safe per-parameter
scalars, no eigendecomposition of the ill-conditioned matrix) unlocks
realistic scales: $\gammaEPL$ acquires a posterior width of $0.037$ (cf.\
the paper's $0.023$) and drifts toward the paper, from $1.866$ at the
mode to $1.746$ in the chain. (iii) A diagonally-scaled-correlation full
Hessian (\texttt{hesscorr}), factored float64-safely as
$\mathrm{chol}(H)=\mathrm{diag}(\sqrt{D})\,\mathrm{chol}(D^{-1/2}HD^{-1/2})$
with $D=\mathrm{diag}(|H_{ii}|)$, restores the correlations that the
diagonal preconditioner drops and lifts the ESS of the {\it correlated}\
mass parameters ($\thetaE$ ESS $8\!\to\!20$, $\gexttwo$ ESS
$3\!\to\!14$). But the correlation matrix $D^{-1/2}HD^{-1/2}$ itself
still has $\mathrm{cond}\sim10^{8}$ --- these are the genuine
strong-lens degeneracies (mass--sheet, slope--ellipticity) and no static
preconditioner removes them.

\paragraph{(5) ESS and multi-chain $\hat R$ characterization.} We ran
both mass-matrix variants as $4$ independent chains each (one per A16
GPU; seeds $0$--$3$; $150$ burn-in $+$ $500$ kept, fixed $16$-step
leapfrog, target acceptance $0.8$), each chain compiling in $\sim$35\,s
and running in $\sim$1\,h of wall time. Acceptance is healthy
($\sim$$0.90$), no chain shows step-size collapse or NaNs, and the
target log-posterior is stable at $\log p\approx-45{,}800$, confirming
that HMC is now sampling the correct mode. The within-chain ESS for the
six mass parameters is small ($\sim$$3$--$20$ out of $300$--$500$
samples), and the between-chain $\hat R$ (Table~\ref{tab:rhat}) is far
above the $1.01$ convergence threshold for most parameters: the chains
have {\it not}\ converged in $500$ samples. The \texttt{diagraw}
$\gammaEPL$ chains span medians $1.37$--$1.86$ (with $\hat R\approx 5$),
which hints at a {\it broad}\ $\gammaEPL$ posterior that could partially
reconcile with the paper's lower slope; testing that breadth is the
purpose of a long $8$-chain \texttt{diagraw} run ($2000$ burn-in $+$
$8000$ kept per chain, $\sim$$17$\,h, $8$-way parallel across the A16s)
that was {\it in progress}\ at the time of writing and whose results
will be folded into a later revision.

\paragraph{Verdict: HMC works mechanically; mixing is
degeneracy-limited.} Every pathology we had previously attributed to
``HMC not working'' is fixed: the kernel compiles in $\sim$35\,s, runs
stably with no step-size collapse, samples a smooth and near-PD mode,
and reproduces the correct posterior scale. The residual is purely
{\it mixing efficiency}: ESS\,$\sim$\,$3$--$20$ per $300$ samples and
$\hat R>1$ at $500$ samples, set by the intrinsic strong-lens parameter
degeneracies ($\mathrm{cond}\sim10^{8}$ that survives diagonal scaling),
not by any sampler defect. Converged inference on this surface would
require $\sim$$10^{4}$--$10^{5}$ samples per chain, which is feasible on
A100-class hardware but is $\sim$$17$--$24$\,h even with $8$-way A16
parallelism --- consistent with the original GIGA-Lens design point of
GPU-resident chains.

\paragraph{The paper's $\gammaEPL=1.372$ mode is a far worse fit in our
setup.} Crucially, the gap to the published slope is {\it not}\ a sampler
artifact. When we refine directly from the paper's reported values
($\gammaEPL=1.372$, $\thetaE=2.646''$), the optimizer stays there
($\gammaEPL=1.374$, $\thetaE=2.640''$) --- it is a genuine local
stationary point of our posterior --- but its log-posterior is
$\approx-78{,}350$, against $\approx-45{,}841$ at our own basin, a
difference of $\Delta\log p\approx-32{,}500$. In our reproduction the
paper-slope configuration is a {\it distinct and dramatically worse}\
fit. We therefore conclude that the $\gammaEPL$ discrepancy reflects an
unpublished {\it model-setup}\ difference --- plausibly the PSF, the
central masking, the source complexity, or the priors
(\S\ref{sec:discussion}) --- rather than a difference in the sampler.
This is consistent with, and sharpens, the multimodality discussion of
\S\ref{sec:multimodal}: our gradient-based machinery now finds and
samples its mode correctly, and that mode is simply not where the
published chain sits.

\subsection{Multimodality of the posterior surface}
\label{sec:multimodal}

The 200-chain v7 multi-start MAP search (TinyTim PSF) returned a set of
clearly distinct local minima. Of the 200 random-prior starts:
\begin{itemize}
\item $17/200 = 8.5\%$ converged to the paper's quadrant
($\eone>0$, $\etwo<0$, $\gextone>0$, $\gexttwo<0$); the best
such chain (idx 140) reached $\chi^{2}=7.72$, $\log p=-2.18\times10^{4}$.
\item $106/200 = 53\%$ had $\etwo<0$ (any sign on the others); the
unconstrained global best ($\chi^{2}=6.67$, $\log p=-1.33\times10^{4}$;
chain idx unspecified at this stage) had $\etwo>0$ and was therefore
{\it not}\ in the paper's quadrant.
\item The top three chains by $\log p$ differed not just in
parameter values but in the {\it sign quadrant}\ of $(\etwo,\gexttwo)$:
the same physical system admits at least two qualitatively distinct
mass-model orientations consistent with the data plus our priors.
\end{itemize}
The paper's chain is therefore not the global best under our
priors and PSF, even though our priors closely follow those of
\citet[][Table 2]{huang2025foundry1}: see Appendix~\ref{app:priors}.

\subsection{Empirical-PSF flip}
\label{sec:psfflip}

Replacing TinyTim with the empirical PSF of \S\ref{sec:psf} produced
a striking change in the topology of the multi-start search. With the
empirical kernel, in the v9 200-chain search:
\begin{itemize}
\item paper-mode chain fraction increased $17/200 \to 45/200$, i.e.\
$8.5\%\to 22.5\%$;
\item the fraction of chains with $\gammaEPL<1.7$ (the paper sits at
$\gammaEPL=1.37$) increased from $\sim 5\%$ to $35\%$
($70/200$ in v9);
\item the {\it global best}\ chain (idx 163, $\chi^{2}=11.93$,
$\log p=-5.64\times10^{4}$) is itself a paper-mode chain.
\end{itemize}
The $\chi^{2}$ floor under the empirical PSF rose from 6.67 to 11.93,
which is what we should expect: TinyTim is over-smoothed relative to
the true F140W PSF (Figure~\ref{fig:psf}), so the model had spurious
freedom to ``earn'' $\chi^{2}$ improvement by absorbing residuals into
the source shapelets. Forcing the model to use a wider, more honest
PSF raises the achievable $\chi^{2}$ floor and re-centers the
posterior on a physically meaningful mode.

The v10 SVI posterior reported in Table~\ref{tab:mass} is the
$10^{4}$-sample Gaussian variational posterior pinned to this v9
global-best paper-mode chain.

\subsection{Coherent residual signature suggests a single missing
constraint}
\label{sec:single-constraint}

The residuals between our v10 posterior and \citet{huang2025foundry1}
Table 3 are not random across the six parameters --- they are coherent
along directions that suggest one underlying physical effect. Specifically:
\begin{itemize}
\item The lens ellipticity vector rotates by $12^{\circ}$
(\eone\ accurate to $2.5\%$, but \etwo\ is $\sim 60\%$ low in magnitude
in a sign-consistent way).
\item The external shear position angle matches to within $1^{\circ}$,
but the magnitude is $\sim 30\%$ low.
\item The deprojected slope \gammaEPL\ runs to a value of 2.15 instead
of the published 1.37, a 57\% increase in slope; in our priors
$\gammaEPL$ has a lower bound at 1.0, and the chain finds a stable
local minimum well above it. \citet{huang2025foundry1} themselves
note (their \S 4) that the published posterior was ``pushing
against the prior lower bound'' --- their reported median sits at
$\gammaEPL=1.37$ with $1\sigma=0.023$, but the prior range starts at
$1.0$, leaving little room.
\end{itemize}
Each of these three effects --- ellipticity-vector rotation, smaller
shear magnitude, higher slope --- is consistent with a model in which
the lens-light and mass-model position angles are not tied, so the
mass model is free to adopt a slightly different orientation than
the lens light and partially absorb the missing constraint into a
mix of $|\etwo|$, $|\gext|$, and \gammaEPL. The paper's mass--light PA
tying (or an equivalent multipole-correction term) would in principle
pin the lens ellipticity to the lens-light position angle and remove
this rotational freedom, reducing the model to a sub-region of our
parameter space where the slope can drift to the lower-\gammaEPL\
mode found by the published HMC.

Testing this hypothesis quantitatively is out of scope for the present
work --- it would require either a code modification to \gigalens\ 2.0
(to expose a mass--light PA tying argument) or a fully custom log-
likelihood that imposes the tying as a soft penalty. Both are natural
next steps for v11 of this reproduction.

\subsection{The PSF is a load-bearing assumption}

Sections~\ref{sec:psf} and \ref{sec:psfflip} together demonstrate that
the choice of PSF is not a cosmetic detail of the pipeline. Replacing
TinyTim with the empirical kernel from in-field stars flipped the
global-best chain into the paper's mode. The mechanism is plausible:
a too-narrow PSF gives the source-shapelet basis more freedom to
absorb central residuals, lowering the model's marginal preference
for a steep ($\gammaEPL\gtrsim 2$) deflector, but only in modes where
the ellipticity vector accommodates the spurious flux. Any future
{\it HST}\ \gigalens\ reproduction --- including the still-pending
Foundry II--V volumes --- should build an empirical PSF from field
stars in the same exposure before fitting.

\subsection{Multimodality is a real feature of the posterior surface}
\label{sec:multimodal_v11}

We now have three independent lines of evidence for genuine
multimodality of the posterior, and consider the multimodality to be
a feature of the model conditioned on our priors and PSF rather than
an artefact of any single inference run.

\paragraph{Evidence (a): v7 multi-start MAP.} The 200-chain v7
multi-start search revealed at least three distinct local minima
differing in the sign quadrant of $(\etwo,\gexttwo)$ at fixed positive
\eone\ and positive \gextone\ (\S\ref{sec:multimodal}); only $17/200
=8.5\%$ of chains land in the paper's quadrant under TinyTim.

\paragraph{Evidence (b): v6 vs v8 mode swap.} The v6 single-chain MAP
(TinyTim, $\chi^{2}=6.59$) sits in a sign quadrant
$(\eone,\etwo,\gextone,\gexttwo)=(+,+,+,+)$ that is different from the
v8 SVI mode $(+,-,+,-)$, even though both converged stably under the
same loss function (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation}). The two are
qualitatively distinct lensing geometries, not refinements of one
another.

\paragraph{Evidence (c): v11f NUTS basin transit.} The v11f NUTS run
(\S\ref{sec:nuts}, \S\ref{sec:nuts_v11f_results}) was initialised at
the v7 paper-mode MAP point and during burn-in moved $\sim 35{,}000$
$\log p$ units into a basin at $\log p\approx -63{,}868$, distinct
from the v10 SVI basin at $\log p\approx -40{,}400$. The medians
at v11f's terminal basin reproduce $\thetaE$ to $1.6\%$ but have a
different signature in $(\eone,\etwo,\gext)$ than v10 does --- in
particular $\eone=+0.159$ (vs.\ v10's $+0.106$) and $\etwo=-0.151$
(vs.\ v10's $-0.053$). This is a third distinct local mode, found
by a gradient-based sampler rather than by the multi-start scan, and
confirms that the model surface admits at least three usable basins
under our priors and PSF.

The published HMC posterior is centered in only one of these basins.
Two interpretations are possible: (i) the published HMC chain explored
other modes but with very low weight, and the reported median is
correctly centered; (ii) the published HMC chain was started in the
paper-mode basin and remained there for the duration of the run.
We cannot distinguish these from the published Foundry I paper alone,
and neither is unreasonable. The implication, however, is the same:
when running \gigalens\ on a new system without an external prior on
ellipticity sign, a multi-start MAP scan {\it followed by}\
mode-aware sampling (or, in lieu of that, transparent reporting of
which local basin the chain occupies) is the right first step ---
gradient-based local search returns a posterior mode, not the
posterior.

\subsection{Upstream issues encountered}
\label{sec:upstream}

We encountered seven issues in the \gigalens\,2.0 / JAX\,0.6.2 /
TFP\,0.25.0 stack that we believe are appropriate to file as GitHub
issues:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Implicit \texttt{shard\_map} import.} \gigalens\,2.0
references \texttt{jax.experimental.shard\_map.shard\_map} by attribute
without first ensuring the submodule is imported. Under JAX 0.6.2 this
fails with \texttt{AttributeError: module 'jax.experimental' has no
attribute 'shard\_map'} unless the caller pre-imports
\texttt{jax.experimental.shard\_map}. We work around this by adding
\texttt{import jax.experimental.shard\_map} to every fitting script.
\item \textbf{Negative lens-light amplitudes via unconstrained
\texttt{lstsq}.} The \gigalens\ simulator path that resolves S\'{e}rsic
intensities via \texttt{jnp.linalg.lstsq} can return negative
amplitudes. These are unphysical, and the resulting MAP can absorb
$\chi^{2}$ at non-physical configurations (Appendix~\ref{app:ablation},
v4 versus v5). A NNLS-based variant or a clipped output would resolve
this. We disable this code path entirely by setting
\texttt{use\_lstsq=False} on every S\'{e}rsic component.
\item \textbf{HMC ``compile time'' at $74$ parameters is really a
\texttt{pmap}$\times$\texttt{lstsq}-convolution$\times$autotuner
pathology.} The gigalens \texttt{HMC()} helper wraps
\texttt{PreconditionedHMC}$+$\texttt{GradientBasedTrajectoryLength}
\texttt{Adaptation} in \texttt{jax.pmap} over all visible devices; on
our $10$-device host, with the \texttt{use\_lstsq=True} $31$-channel
grouped convolution, the cuDNN autotuner thrashes (one algorithm alone
took $11$\,min\,$50$\,s), which is the multi-hour ``hang.'' The one-step
kernel in fact compiles in $31$--$38$\,s on a single device for {\it all}\
of HMC$+$GBTLA, fixed-leapfrog HMC, and NUTS (\S\ref{sec:hmc},
\texttt{26\_compile\_diagnostic.py}). The remedy is to run on a single
or few devices and set
\texttt{XLA\_FLAGS=--xla\_gpu\_autotune\_level=0}; gigalens would benefit
from not defaulting to a global \texttt{pmap} and from exposing the
autotune level. (As a stop-gap before this diagnosis,
\texttt{PreconditionedNoUTurnSampler} with a static
\texttt{max\_tree\_depth} and no \texttt{pmap}, \S\ref{sec:nuts}, also
sidesteps the blowup, compiling in $\sim$5\,min.)
\item \textbf{\texttt{lstsq\_simulate} omits the Gaussian-evidence term
and is unregularized, making the profiled posterior non-smooth.}
gigalens's \texttt{lstsq\_simulate} (\texttt{gigalens.jax.simulator})
solves the linear amplitudes via $\mathrm{pinv}(X^{\!\top}WX)$,
{\it omits}\ the $-\tfrac12\log\det(X^{\!\top}WX)$ Gaussian-evidence
normalization, and applies no prior regularization. On the
near-rank-deficient shapelet design matrix the \texttt{pinv}
singular-value truncation makes the profiled log-posterior non-smooth in
the nonlinear parameters, so \texttt{jax.grad} floors at
$\|\nabla\|\sim10^{5}$ and no PD mode is reachable (\S\ref{sec:hmc}).
Adding the $-\tfrac12\log\det$ term and a prior-derived Tikhonov ridge
$\Lambda$ (so the solve is a Cholesky on $X^{\!\top}WX+\Lambda$) restores
smoothness and is the fix we implement in \texttt{\_hmc\_lib\_marg.py}.
\item \textbf{Mixed-GPU-type \texttt{shard\_map} crash.} On heterogeneous
hosts (e.g.\ our 8$\times$A16 $+$ 2$\times$L4), the executable
compiled for the first sharded device cannot be run on the others, and
the shard map crashes mid-run with
\texttt{executable is built for device CUDA:0 of type 'NVIDIA L4'; cannot
run on CUDA:2 of type 'NVIDIA A16'}. UUID-pinning the visible devices
is a workaround; a cleaner fix would be for the shard map machinery
to recompile per-device or to refuse to shard across heterogeneous
device types with an actionable error.
\item \textbf{\texttt{jnp.linalg.cholesky} silently returns NaN on
a rank-deficient SVI covariance.} The v10 SVI posterior covariance
has $\min\lambda(qz\_cov)=-4.4\times10^{-13}$ and numerical rank
$53/74$ (\S\ref{sec:nuts}). On this matrix the CPU NumPy Cholesky
raises \texttt{NotPositiveDefinite}, but the JAX Cholesky silently
returns NaN. NUTS then receives NaN momentum and proceeds without
erroring, producing the appearance of a stuck chain rather than a
diagnosable failure. At minimum, \texttt{jnp.linalg.cholesky} should
emit a NaN-trace warning under \texttt{jax\_debug\_nans} the way other
NaN-producing JAX primitives do. The downstream \gigalens\ pipeline
would benefit from a defensive \texttt{cov + eps*I} jitter in any code
path that feeds an SVI covariance into a TFP preconditioner.
\item \textbf{\texttt{DualAveragingStepSizeAdaptation} pathology on
multimodal-Hessian models.} On this 74-parameter model the dual-averaging
adapter consistently drove the step size to $\sim$2--$3\times10^{-6}$
within 200 burn-in steps, at which acceptance is $1.000$ but per-step
$\|\Delta z\|$ is $10^{-4}$--$10^{-3}$ and the chain effectively does
not move (\S\ref{sec:nuts}). A static log-spaced scan from $10^{-5}$
to $3\times10^{-2}$ with adaptation disabled gives $0\%$ acceptance
everywhere, so the working step size lives in the narrow window
$(2\times10^{-6},10^{-5})$ --- below the resolution of a coarse scan
and below where dual-averaging settles. We interpret this as the
adapter optimizing a single global step-size for a target acceptance
under a globally Gaussian-like assumption that the model surface
violates: the local Hessian at the v10 SVI mode and the local Hessian
at the v11f basin differ enough that no single mass matrix or step
size is simultaneously appropriate. A windowed mass-matrix adapter
(e.g.\ \texttt{windowed\_adaptive\_nuts}) or a per-basin re-tuning
would in principle help; both require a \texttt{JointDistribution}
refactor of \gigalens\ which we defer.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{What full reproduction would require}

To reproduce the published HMC posterior bit-for-bit one would need
(i) the paper's exact \gigalens\ git revision (the multi-node branch
of \texttt{giga-lens/gigalens} has moved since the paper was written);
(ii) mass--light position-angle tying, which is not exposed in the
\gigalens\,2.0 public API as of our run date and would require a
modification to the simulator internals; (iii) optionally a
higher-order multipole correction term in the EPL profile;
(iv) enough sampling to converge: we now have a mechanically working HMC
at the marginalized $46$-parameter model (\S\ref{sec:hmc}; it compiles in
$\sim$35\,s and samples the correct mode), but the surface is so
degenerate ($\mathrm{cond}\sim10^{8}$ after diagonal scaling) that
converged inference needs $\sim$$10^{4}$--$10^{5}$ samples per chain,
$\sim$$17$--$24$\,h on the A16s even $8$-way parallel and best run on
A100-class hardware; and (v) a \texttt{JointDistribution} refactor of the
\gigalens\ prob model to enable \texttt{windowed\_adaptive\_nuts} or
similar window-adaptive mass-matrix samplers, which would improve mixing
efficiency on the correlated mass directions. We stress that the
$\gammaEPL$ gap is {\it not}\ closed by any of these: in our setup the
paper's $\gammaEPL=1.372$ configuration is a genuine but far worse local
fit ($\Delta\log p\approx-32{,}500$ relative to our basin,
\S\ref{sec:hmc}), so item (i)--(iii) --- the load-bearing PSF, masking,
and prior choices --- are what a bit-for-bit reproduction actually turns
on. These are the natural next-steps for any reader who wishes to push
this reproduction further.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}
%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%%

We have presented an end-to-end public-data reproduction of the
\gigalens\ modeling demonstration of Foundry I \citep{huang2025foundry1}
on the system \foundry. The principal findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item All six mass-model sign quadrants of the published HMC posterior
are reproduced by our SVI Gaussian variational posterior.
\item \thetaE\ matches the published median to $3.0\%$; \eone\ to
$2.5\%$; the external-shear position angle to within $1^{\circ}$.
\item The remaining gap --- a $12^{\circ}$ residual rotation of the
lens ellipticity, $\sim30\%$ smaller $|\etwo|$ and $|\gext|$, and a
$57\%$ steeper \gammaEPL\ --- is coherent rather than scattered, and
is consistent with one missing physical constraint (most likely
mass--light PA tying or a higher-multipole correction) rather than
three independent failures.
\item The Foundry I posterior surface is genuinely multimodal under
the published priors: in 200 random-start MAP fits with TinyTim PSF,
only $8.5\%$ of chains land in the paper's quadrant, and the
unconstrained global best lies in a different ($\etwo>0$) mode.
\item Replacing the gigalens-default TinyTim PSF with an empirical
PSF built from in-field stars flips the global-best chain into the
paper's quadrant ($45/200=22.5\%$, with global best in mode) at the
cost of a higher but more honest $\chi^{2}$ floor.
\item We resolve the upstream HMC JIT-compile blocker by switching
to \texttt{PreconditionedNoUTurnSampler} with a static
\texttt{max\_tree\_depth} and no \texttt{pmap} wrapper, but the
\texttt{DualAveragingStepSizeAdaptation} adapter collapses on this
model: the local-curvature mismatch between modes prevents a single
mass matrix or step size from working, and a windowed mass-matrix
adapter would require a \texttt{JointDistribution} refactor of the
\gigalens\ prob model. The v11f NUTS run, started from the v7
paper-mode MAP point, converges into a {\it third}\ distinct local
mode at $\log p\approx -63{,}868$ with $\thetaE=2.604''$ ($1.6\%$ off
the published value, our closest reproduction of \thetaE), confirming
that the posterior surface is genuinely multimodal but not yielding a
mixed chain.
\item We document six upstream software issues in \gigalens\,2.0/
JAX\,0.6.2/TFP\,0.25.0 that would, if resolved, materially improve
the reproducibility of any Foundry-lineage analysis --- notably the
silent NaN from \texttt{jnp.linalg.cholesky} on a rank-deficient SVI
covariance, and the dual-averaging step-size pathology on
multimodal-Hessian models.
\end{enumerate}

For the broader DESI Strong Lens Foundry program, and for the
GIGA-Lens lineage at large, these findings argue that an
in-exposure empirical PSF and a multi-start MAP scan are reasonable
defaults to add to the published recipe, that mass--light PA tying
or an equivalent multipole correction is a worthwhile next-order
modeling improvement, that NUTS with static \texttt{max\_tree\_depth}
should replace HMC as the default JIT-tractable sampler at this model
size, and that mode-aware adaptation (windowed mass-matrix estimation,
basin-conditional re-tuning, or transparent reporting of the basin a
chain occupies) is a prerequisite for trustworthy posterior inference
at LSST/Roman/Euclid scale.
